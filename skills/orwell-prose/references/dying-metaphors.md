# Dying metaphors — flag list

Worn figures of speech used with no image in the writer's mind. Flag them under **Rule 1**. The
fix is rarely "invent a new metaphor" — usually it's **cut to the literal meaning**. Only reach
for a fresh image if it genuinely earns its place.

**Tell-tale signs the metaphor is dead, not live:**
- The writer gets the wording wrong because the picture is gone: *tow the line* (for *toe*),
  *hone in* (for *home in*), *for all intensive purposes*, *escape goat*.
- Mixed/colliding metaphors: *"a virgin field pregnant with possibilities"*, *"the Achilles'
  heel of the cornerstone"*, *"let's not open a can of worms that opens a Pandora's box."*
- The figure is used in a sense that contradicts its own image (Orwell's *hammer and anvil*: the
  phrase implies the anvil always *gets* the worst of it, which is the opposite of fact).

## Orwell's original examples
*Ring the changes on · take up the cudgels for · toe the line · ride roughshod over · stand
shoulder to shoulder with · play into the hands of · an axe to grind · grist to the mill · fishing
in troubled waters · on the order of the day · Achilles' heel · swan song · hotbed.*

## Common modern dying metaphors to flag
*The elephant in the room · move the needle · low-hanging fruit · boil the ocean · push the
envelope · think outside the box · drink the Kool-Aid · the 800-pound gorilla · level the playing
field · the perfect storm · a paradigm shift · open the kimono · run it up the flagpole · circle
back · take it offline · drill down · hit the ground running · move the goalposts · the tip of
the iceberg · raise the bar · a game-changer · at the end of the day · when the rubber meets the
road · all hands on deck · boots on the ground · the new normal · a deep dive · in the weeds ·
ducks in a row · the light at the end of the tunnel · a double-edged sword · the writing on the
wall · a slippery slope · the bottom line.*

## How to flag

Quote the phrase, tag it `[dying metaphor]`, and offer the literal meaning:
- ❌ "We need to move the needle on engagement." → ✅ "We need more people to use it."
- ❌ "Let's circle back on the low-hanging fruit." → ✅ "Let's talk later about the easy wins"
  — or, if *easy wins* is itself stale here, "the changes we can ship this week."

Note when a metaphor is genuinely **live and earning its keep** — do not strip imagery from
writing that is using it deliberately and freshly. The target is the *automatic* idiom reached
for to dodge thought, not vividness itself.
