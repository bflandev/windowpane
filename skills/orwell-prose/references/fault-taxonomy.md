# The four faults — full taxonomy

Orwell's catalog of "tricks by means of which the work of prose-construction is habitually
dodged." All four share one root: **the writer is not pinning a precise meaning to precise words.**
Either they have a meaning and can't express it, or they don't much care what they mean, or they
are actively hiding it. The last is the dangerous one.

> "The whole tendency of modern prose is away from concreteness." — Orwell

> "The great enemy of clear language is insincerity. When there is a gap between one's real and
> one's declared aims, one turns as it were instinctively to long words and exhausted idioms,
> like a cuttlefish squirting out ink." — Orwell

> "If thought corrupts language, language can also corrupt thought." — Orwell

---

## 1. Dying metaphors

A live metaphor draws a picture; a dead one (e.g. *iron resolution*) has fused into an ordinary
word. The trouble is the **dying** metaphor in between: a worn figure used with no image in the
writer's mind, kept only because it saves the trouble of inventing a phrase. Tell: the writer
often gets it *wrong* because the picture is gone — "tow the line" for *toe the line*, mixed
metaphors ("the Achilles' heel of the foundation"), figures used in self-contradiction.

→ Full flaggable list in `dying-metaphors.md`. Fix: cut to the literal meaning, or invent a fresh
image only if one genuinely helps (Rule 1).

## 2. Operators / verbal false limbs

The writer replaces a single verb with a phrase — a noun or adjective tacked onto a general-
purpose verb (*prove, serve, form, play, render*). This pads sentences and gives "an air of
scientific impartiality to biased judgements."

- **Phrase-for-verb:** *render inoperative, militate against, make contact with, be subjected to,
  give rise to, give grounds for, have the effect of, play a leading part/role in, make itself
  felt, take effect, exhibit a tendency to, serve the purpose of.*
- **Passive preferred to active**, and noun constructions preferred to gerunds (*by examination
  of* rather than *by examining*).
- **"-ize" and "de-" formations** manufacture verbs (*operationalize, prioritize,
  incentivize*).
- **The "not un-" formation** dresses a banal statement as profound (*not unjustifiable, not
  unlike*). Orwell's cure: memorize "A not unblack dog was chasing a not unsmall rabbit across a
  not ungreen field."
- **Padding connectives** swell a sentence with the appearance of logic: *with respect to,
  having regard to, the fact that, in view of, in the interests of, on the hypothesis that, by
  dint of.*

→ Full operator → plain-verb table in `false-limbs.md`. Fix: find the buried verb and use it.

## 3. Pretentious diction

Words used to **dress up** simple statements, lend false scientific weight, or glorify. Three
sub-types:

- **Long Latinate over short Saxon:** *phenomenon, element, individual (n.), objective,
  categorical, effective, virtual, basic, primary, constitute, exhibit, exploit, utilize,
  eliminate, liquidate, ameliorate, predict.* Almost always a plainer English word exists
  (*use* for *utilize*, *make/form* for *constitute*).
- **Inflated adjectives** glamorize the ordinary: *epoch-making, epic, historic, unforgettable,
  triumphant, age-old, inevitable, inexorable, veritable.*
- **War/grandeur vocabulary & needless foreign phrases** dignify squalid things or flatter the
  writer: *realm, throne, mailed fist, jackboot, clarion*; *deus ex machina, mutatis mutandis,
  status quo, weltanschauung, ancien régime.* "Bad writers... are nearly always haunted by the
  notion that Latin or Greek words are grander than Saxon ones."

→ Lists in `false-limbs.md`. Fix: the everyday English equivalent (Rule 5), unless the precise
term has no plain synonym.

## 4. Meaningless words

Words that have **no agreed meaning** and are used either because the writer has only a vague
feeling, or — worse — to deceive. Two arenas:

- **Art/criticism:** *romantic, plastic, values, human, dead, sentimental, natural, vitality* —
  "not only do they not point to any discoverable object, but they are hardly ever expected to
  do so."
- **Politics:** *democracy, socialism, freedom, patriotic, realistic, justice, fascism.* Each has
  several irreconcilable meanings; "fascism" has come to mean simply "something not desirable."
  These are "often used in a consciously dishonest way" — the speaker has a private definition but
  lets the hearer think something else.

The **modern corporate descendants** to flag: *synergy, leverage (v.), world-class, best-in-class,
robust, holistic, value-add, mission-critical, paradigm, ecosystem, empower, disrupt, solutions,
stakeholder alignment, going forward, at the end of the day.* Tell: you cannot replace the word
with a concrete picture or test. Ask "what would this look like / measure as, in practice?" — if
there's no answer, it's meaningless.

Fix (editor): cut it, or force the writer to name the concrete thing meant. Fix (detector): name
the gap between the word and any reality behind it.

---

## The single diagnostic question

For any suspect phrase, Orwell's test is: **can I picture it, or measure it, or point to it?**
Concrete language survives this; the four faults do not. Pair it with: **"Could I put it more
shortly? Have I said anything that is avoidably ugly?"**
