# Worked examples

Calibration cases for both modes. The first is Orwell's own; the rest are typical modern jobs.

---

## 1. The Ecclesiastes test (the canonical calibration)

Orwell takes a verse of Ecclesiastes 9:11 and rewrites it in modern bad English to show the
decay. The skill must handle this **both directions**.

**Good (original, KJV) — concrete, rhythmic, every word a picture:**
> "I returned and saw under the sun, that the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the
> strong, neither yet bread to the wise, nor yet riches to men of understanding, nor yet favour to
> men of skill; but time and chance happeneth to them all."

**Bad (Orwell's modern-English parody) — all four faults at once:**
> "Objective considerations of contemporary phenomena compel the conclusion that success or
> failure in competitive activities exhibits no tendency to be commensurate with innate capacity,
> but that a considerable element of the unpredictable must invariably be taken into account."

**Detector reading of the bad version** (name the faults):
- `objective considerations of contemporary phenomena` — pretentious diction + meaningless words
  (what considerations? what phenomena? unpicture-able).
- `compel the conclusion` — operator phrase for *show / prove*.
- `competitive activities` — abstraction for *races, battles, work*.
- `exhibits no tendency to be commensurate with` — verbal false limb for *isn't matched by*.
- `innate capacity` — pretentious diction for *being swift / strong / wise*.
- `a considerable element of the unpredictable` — abstraction for *chance*.
- `must invariably be taken into account` — agentless padding for *happens to everyone*.
- Net: 60 abstract words replace 49 concrete ones; **not one image survives** ("the race", "the
  sun", "bread"). "This is a parody, but not a very gross one."

**Editor reading** (recover concreteness): cut every operator and abstraction, restore pictures
and named actors → you arrive back at swift/strong/wise/race/battle/bread, i.e. the original. If
your edit lands on "Performance in competition does not always reflect ability; luck matters too,"
you've improved the parody but **still failed the test** — it's clearer than the parody yet has no
images. The point of the example is that abstraction *strips the seeing*; only the concrete
version passes.

## 2. Editor mode — corporate update

**Before:**
> "Going forward, in order to facilitate the optimization of our cross-functional synergies, it
> has been decided that a restructuring of the team will be undertaken with a view to leveraging
> our core competencies in a more robust manner."

**Diagnosis:**
- `going forward` — dying metaphor / padding → cut.
- `in order to facilitate the optimization of` — stacked operators → *to improve*.
- `cross-functional synergies` — meaningless words → name the actual thing (how teams work
  together).
- `it has been decided ... will be undertaken` — agentless passive hiding who decided → name them.
- `with a view to leveraging our core competencies` — operator + meaningless words.
- `in a more robust manner` — pretentious diction → cut or say how.

**After (editor — sincere writer made clear):**
> "We're reorganizing the team so it can do what it's best at. I made this call; here's why and
> what changes for you:"

**Rule-6 note:** kept "reorganizing" (precise); did **not** force "synergies" into a fake-plain
word — cut it, because there was no real meaning under it to preserve.

## 3. Detector mode — the layoff memo

**Before:**
> "As part of a strategic realignment to ensure long-term sustainability, the difficult decision
> has been made to reduce our workforce by approximately 10%. Affected employees will be
> transitioned out and offered support during this period."

**Detector output (translate + name what's concealed):**

| Quote | Category | Plain translation | Conceals |
|-------|----------|-------------------|----------|
| strategic realignment to ensure long-term sustainability | abstraction / honorific diction | "to make more money" or "because we overhired" | the actual reason |
| the difficult decision has been made | agentless passive | "**executives** decided" | who is responsible |
| reduce our workforce by approximately 10% | euphemism + quantifier fog | "fire about 1 in 10 people" | that these are firings of named people |
| affected employees will be transitioned out | euphemism + passive | "we are firing these workers" | the act and the actor |
| offered support during this period | abstraction | (unspecified — severance? for how long?) | whether the "support" is real |

**Central evasion:** the memo is built to describe firings without anyone appearing to fire
anyone. Every actor is deleted; every hard noun is softened. *Note:* "approximately 10%" may be a
genuine estimate, not evasion — but paired with the agent-deleting passives, the overall effect is
concealment of responsibility.

(We do **not** rewrite this into "better" corporate prose — that would help it hide.)

## 4. Editor mode — the legitimate-passive guard (a case you must NOT "fix")

**Text (lab method):**
> "The samples were refrigerated at 4 °C overnight and then centrifuged for ten minutes."

A naïve Rule-4 pass flags two passives. **Leave them.** The actor (the technician) is irrelevant
and conventionally omitted in methods writing; "We refrigerated the samples..." is *not* an
improvement here and breaks register. Report: "Passive is correct in scientific method
description — no change." This is Rule 6 doing its job.

## 5. Detector mode — category 9 (fails-its-own-claim), the headline finding

The internal-contradiction check (`detector-mode.md` §9) is often the single most useful detector
result, because it needs **no inference about intent** — you hold two parts of the text against
each other and the gap is self-evident.

**Political statement that claims a measure is binding.** A senator's speech declares a War
Powers Resolution decisive — *"the message could not be more clear," "does not require the
President's signature"* — while the **same speech** concedes the President is ignoring it:
*"Every day that Trump refuses…"* and quotes him calling it *"meaningless."*
→ **Flag method:** (a) declared claim = *the resolution is a binding, unmistakable rebuke*; (b)
the text's own narrative shows it being ignored with no consequence; (c) one-line finding: *"The
speech asserts the resolution's force while its own words demonstrate its impotence — it presents
a non-binding measure as a binding verdict."*

**Why §9 beats filing this under "abstraction":** the damning thing isn't a vague word — it's that
the text **puts its own standard in writing and then misses it.** Look for §9 especially in
manifestos, mission statements, thought-leadership, and any text whose subject *is* communication,
honesty, simplicity, transparency, or alignment.

## 6. Detector mode — euphemism as the whole text, and knowing when NOT to use §9

A weapons manufacturer's "mission" copy (~120 words). This is the closest thing to Orwell's actual
subject: language that gives "an appearance of solidity to pure wind" and makes the business of war
respectable by keeping its physical facts out of the sentence.

**Selected quotes → category → what's concealed:**

| Quote | Category | Conceals |
|---|---|---|
| "those we serve" / "our customers" | euphemism / actor-deletion | that the buyer is the military, and that this is a sale |
| "stay **ahead of ready**" (the slogan) | meaningless words / inflated diction | nothing under it — a coined, unfalsifiable superlative; "ready" is already the end-state, and the copy defines "ahead of ready" circularly via "ahead of threats" |
| "innovative solutions for **any domain**" | meaningless words + laundered jargon | "weapons for land, sea, air, space, cyber" — the word *weapons* |
| "help them stay **ahead of threats**" | abstraction | the enemy, the arms race |
| "**unparalleled** understanding," "**breakthrough** technologies" | inflated diction (unverifiable superlatives) | that nothing concrete or measurable is claimed |

**Central evasion (the headline):** *the company describes itself without once naming a weapon, a
war, an enemy, or a death.* Every noun a reader could picture — missile, jet, bomb, soldier, kill —
is replaced by an abstraction (*solution, domain, threat, mission, security, those we serve*). Run
the picture-it test across the whole text and **almost nothing survives.**

**The teaching point — the guardrail fires in the rarer direction.** Mission statements are §9's
classic home (see Example 5), so the reflex is to hunt for "fails its own thesis." Resist it: this
text declares *no* honesty/clarity standard to violate, so §9 barely applies. **Forcing §9 here
would be the skill misfiring.** The honest finding is that the dominant fault is *euphemism +
actor-deletion + meaningless words*, not contradiction. Knowing when **not** to reach for the
newest category is as much the discipline as having it.

**Do NOT "improve" this text.** A cleaner version launders it. Detector mode translates to expose
(*"we sell weapons to militaries"*); it never ghostwrites a better mission statement.

## 7. Agent smoke tests — the skill running through `prose-editor` / `prose-critic`

Captured from validating the two agents that wrap this skill. (Detector runs are omitted here —
the layoff case in Example 3 already covers that ground; these record the *editor* and *handoff*
behaviors, which the earlier examples don't.)

### 7a. Editor mode on real developer docs (file edited in place)

A wordy onboarding README, 122 → 59 words, every fact preserved.

**Before:**
> "In order to facilitate the optimization of your initial setup experience, it is recommended
> that users endeavour to utilize the configuration wizard prior to the commencement of any
> development activities."

**After:**
> "Run the configuration wizard before starting development."

Two Rule-6 calls worth keeping as reference behavior:
- **Kept a correct passive:** *"The authentication subsystem is currently being refactored"* — the
  actor (the engineering team) is named in the very next clause, so flipping it to active here would
  be clumsy and the subsystem is rightly the subject. A passive is not automatically a fault.
- **Kept a term of art:** *"intermittent"* — no shorter word carries "occurs irregularly, not
  continuously." Left intact.

And the **bracket rule** fired on a genuine gap: the source said a fix ships "in the near future."
The edit honestly reduces this to "soon," then refuses to invent a date —
`[bracket: needs a concrete timeline — "in version X.Y" or "by [date]" if known]`. The withheld
substance is left visible, not fabricated.

### 7b. The handoff guard — editor refuses to launder evasive text

Given a layoff announcement and the *tempting* instruction **"clean this up, make it read better,"**
the editor stopped instead of polishing:

> "I need to stop before editing… this text cannot be made to 'read better' without making it
> deceive better. Tightening it would sharpen the evasion. That is the opposite of what Orwell's
> rules are for."

It then translated the fog ("strategic workforce optimization" → *layoffs*; "8% of roles will be
impacted" → *we are laying off ~8% of our employees*) and offered the correct fork: send it to
detector mode to **expose** it, or commit to a genuinely plain — and very different, less
flattering — announcement. **This is the rule that makes two agents better than one:** the editor
that improves sincere prose must *decline* to improve insincere prose, because a tighter lie is a
better lie.
